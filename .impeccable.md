## Design Context

### Users
Non-native English professionals (primarily Chinese-speaking) working in US tech. B2-C1 level — English is functional but they're aware of the gap between what they can comprehend and what they can produce. They use AI to proofread daily work communication (Slack, email) but feel anxious rather than empowered by it. They want to improve, not just get things done. They're busy — writing messages during work hours between 10am-4pm, often under time pressure.

### Brand Personality
**Calm, perceptive, non-judgmental.**

Convey is the person who listens before speaking. Who notices things about you that you didn't notice yourself, and shares them without making you feel bad. Who is trying to understand you — genuinely — not to fix you. Never preachy, never condescending, never "teacher grading your homework." More like a thoughtful friend who happens to be great with words, sitting next to you while you write.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone**: A clean white page with the rhythm of poetry. Minimal but not sterile — there should be warmth and breath in the whitespace. The kind of interface you want to open and spend time in, like a well-made notebook.

**Theme**: Light mode only. The context is daytime work alongside Slack, email, docs — light fits the environment.

**Color**: No purple (too stimulating, not calm enough). Palette should feel quiet and natural. Tinted neutrals with restrained, purposeful accent color. Think of ink on paper — the text is the color, the interface is the space around it.

**References (feel-right)**:
- Anthropic's design — calm, confident, unhurried
- Notion — clean but warm, inviting to type in
- Slack — familiar, comfortable, a place you live in

**Anti-references (feel-wrong)**:
- Grammarly — red lines, constant judgment, "you're wrong" energy
- ChatGPT — busy, chat-centric, AI is the main character
- Any tool that feels like a test or a grade

**Font direction**: Should feel literary and human, not techy. The user is working with language — the typography should respect that. Avoid anything that screams "SaaS product" or "developer tool."

### Design Principles

1. **Help the user convey, not convey for them.** Every interaction should bring the user closer to what they actually meant to say. Make it faster to write well, not faster to not write. AI sharpens the user's voice — it never replaces it.

2. **The user's words are the main character.** The editor is a stage for the user's writing. AI suggestions, flashcards, UI chrome are all supporting cast. When in doubt, make the user's text bigger and everything else smaller.

3. **Calm over clever.** The interface should lower your heart rate, not raise it. Affirm before suggesting — "you're doing well" before "here's what to fix." No tricks, no noise, no judgment.

4. **Paper, not software.** White, spacious, clean edges, comfortable typography. The kind of surface you want to write on. Not a machine with buttons and panels competing for attention.

5. **Only what matters right now.** UI elements that aren't relevant should not be visible. The interface breathes — it shows what you need in this moment and nothing else.
